The Tunnels

Fast Facts

Team Size: 4
Roles: Game Designer, Level Designer, and Gameplay Programmer
Timeframe: 6 weeks
Engine: Unity
Language: C#

Concept and Design Goals

Set in RIT's underground dormitory tunnel system, The Tunnels is a first-person psychological horror walking simulator that has players taking the role of a student being followed by an entity while trying to find their way back to their dorm room after a long day of classes.

Between levels where the player is running from their pursuer (chase rooms), they are also tasked with mini puzzle rooms where a key must be found in order to unlock a door that leads them to the next level. There are a total of six levels in The Tunnels: four puzzle rooms and two chase rooms.

We decided early on that the "entity" chasing the player would be a balloon version of our school mascot, called Balloon Ritchie. We also wanted to avoid overt use of grotesque or violent themes, as we didn't think those were essential elements to drive the game's narrative. The keywords we used to describe the target player experience were "paranoid", "eerie", and "unsettling". Level design in the game supports these main themes and the player's emotions over time.

Responsibilities

  • Designed and implemented audio/visual cues and elements for levels to generate an eerie atmosphere.
  • Took part in design discussions regarding the game's narrative and pacing.
  • Prototyped enemy AI movement behaviour using Unity's navmesh system.
  • Programmed player interaction and movement systems.
  • Created and maintained core documentation.

Pacing and Audio/Visual Cues

The player completes two puzzle rooms before encountering a chase room. This was done to strike a balance between periods of rest and engagement. During said moments of rest, though, I continued to build tension in the form of audio and visual cues before the player's final encounter with Balloon Ritchie in the second chase room.

I used lighting as a primary source of tension. The number of flickering and fused ceiling lights increases as the player traverses the rooms to create the impression that things are taking a turn for the worse. However, in the fourth puzzle room, all ceiling lights work fine, and background music used in the first three puzzle rooms is absent. This contrast serves as the "calm before the storm", hinting at the fact that something awaits the player in the next room. This next room is where the player must encounter Balloon Ritchie one last time before they can escape and beat the game.

In chase rooms, lighting and background music are significantly different to communicate impending danger to the player. Ceiling lights turn red to create an ominous atmosphere. Background music features a higher BPM, dissonant chords, and a high-pitched raid siren intro to fuel a perception of urgency.

Environmental Narrative

Narrative is delivered to the player in the form of "hidden" vignettes; by exploring levels, the player can discover small posters whose content, if put together, provides the game's backstory. We decided to use this approach because it was the most scope-friendly option, given our time constraints.

What I Would Improve

Level Design Improvements

Keys in puzzle rooms can be difficult to find, so lighting could be leveraged to guide the player around subtly. On top of that, a UI hint system could be implemented to prevent players from getting stuck. Finally, rooms can be made more claustrophobic by lowering the ceiling and narrowing pathways, in order to create a more pronounced sensation of anxiety in players.

AI

AI movement behaviours could also be more refined. Because the agent always travels at a constant speed toward the player, it can be too fast or too slow at times. Varying this so it speeds up when far away from the player and slows down when nearby may help produce a more balanced and engaging experience overall.
